ABOUT MAHAKASH

Mahakash (Sanskrit for "The Great Space" — yes, we Googled it) is India's 
most ambitiously optimistic private space company. We build satellites that 
mostly work, rockets that usually go up, and space suits that look absolutely 
fabulous.

Founded in a garage in Koramangala (which we now call "Launch Pad Alpha"), 
Mahakash has grown from 3 sleep-deprived engineers and a whiteboard to over 
80 employees across 10 departments, including the legendary "Astro-Catering" 
division, which has never once failed to deliver samosas to mission control 
on launch day.

MISSION

To democratize space access for every Indian business, from telecom giants 
to the chai wallah who just wants better GPS for his delivery route.

VISION

A future where "I'll send you a satellite" is a normal thing to say in 
a business meeting.

VALUES

1. AUDACITY — If no one laughs at your timeline, you're not dreaming big enough.
2. JUGAAD — When NASA uses $50M test equipment, we use an auto-rickshaw 
   chassis and a prayer. Same results. Mostly.
3. CHAI-DRIVEN DEVELOPMENT — All critical engineering decisions are made 
   between the 3rd and 5th chai of the day. Before 3rd chai: no cognition. 
   After 5th chai: too much vibration for precision work.
4. SAFETY FIRST (USUALLY) — We have never lost an employee. We have, however, 
   lost 3 interns in the cafeteria. They were found 6 hours later in the 
   testing lab, still debugging.

KEY PRODUCTS

• MahaSat-1: Our flagship communication satellite. Currently orbiting Earth  
  at 36,000 km and streaming exactly 847 channels of cricket.

• MahaSat-Nano: A satellite so small it was accidentally mailed to a client  
  in a Flipkart box. Still worked.

• Pushpak Mk-I: Our first reusable launch vehicle. "Reusable" in the sense  
  that we reuse the design drawings after each landing attempt.

• Pushpak Mk-II: Learned from Mk-I. Now the rocket actually comes back.  
  Usually to the right landing pad.

• Akash Shield: Our space debris tracking system. Because apparently we're  
  not the only ones leaving junk up there.

• Orbit Express: Rideshare launches for small satellites. Basically Ola for  
  space. "Your satellite has arrived. Rating: ⭐⭐⭐⭐"

NOTABLE ACHIEVEMENTS

• 2020: Successfully launched a test rocket. It was supposed to go 10 km. 
  It went 10.3 km. We celebrate this extra 300 meters every year.

• 2021: MahaSat-Nano deployed for AgriSat Corp. Farmers now get weather 
  data 40% faster. Crop yields up 15%. Samosa consumption at Mahakash 
  HQ also up 15% (correlation unclear).

• 2022: Won ISRO contract for 3 communication satellites. Office party 
  lasted 72 hours. Productivity dipped. Worth it.

• 2023: Pushpak Mk-II achieved first successful vertical landing. The 
  cheer from mission control registered on nearby seismographs.

• 2024: Revenue crossed ₹500 crore. Finance department finally got a 
  second whiteboard.

• 2025: 80+ employees. International clients. Still can't fix the 
  office WiFi in Building B.

UNACKNOWLEDGED (BUT LEGENDARY) ACHIEVEMENTS

• The "BrahMos" Incident (2022): While testing the navigation system of a 
  classified Pushpak payload, a glitch caused the prototype to alter its 
  trajectory and accidentally drop into Pakistan. To our immense relief, the 
  media and international community assumed it was an accidental firing of a 
  BrahMos missile. The Indian Army (bless them) took the blame, a completely 
  innocent wing commander got fired, and our CTO hasn't slept properly since. 
  We sent the officer an anonymous fruit basket.

• The "Sentinel Island" Drop (2023): Our very first attempt at a sub-orbital 
  capsule drop test. It was supposed to be uncrewed, but a Ground Ops technician 
  named Ramesh had sneaked inside to take a nap. Upon reentry, the capsule got 
  caught in an unexpected jet stream and landed smack in the middle of North 
  Sentinel Island in the Andamans. We couldn't retrieve it. We are reasonably 
  sure the Sentinelese now worship Ramesh as a sky god in a spacesuit. 
  HR has marked his status as "On Permanent Deputation."

• The ISRO "Surprise Security Audit" (2024): To demonstrate the robustness 
  of government cyber-defenses, our youngest intern accidentally linked our 
  cafeteria smart-fridge to ISRO's primary staging telemetry server. For 14 
  tense hours, ISRO's mission control received live temperature updates of our 
  paneer tikka instead of their cryogenic engine data. Rather than seeing this 
  as a catastrophic security incident, we view it as a "proactive cross-agency 
  thermal synchronization." ISRO physically cut the fiberline to our building, 
  but they now know precisely who to call for world-class IoT refrigeration.
